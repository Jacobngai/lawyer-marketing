## Chapter 1: The Reach Problem

Networking is valuable. Attending events is valuable. Speaking at conferences and industry gatherings is valuable. The conversation that led to this entire book happened because a lawyer showed up to a BNI event and struck up a conversation with a mutual contact of mine.

But networking has a ceiling, and it is worth understanding exactly where that ceiling sits.

When I was invited to speak as a keynote on AI at an industry event, the room held a few hundred people. A well-attended conference in Malaysia might draw a thousand. A major national event might bring in five thousand. The absolute upper end — a truly exceptional speaker, a truly exceptional stage — might reach ten thousand people in a single day.

Malaysia has thirty-three million people.

Even if you spoke at every industry event in the country for a full year, you would reach a small fraction of your potential market. And of those people, almost none of them would need a lawyer at that exact moment. You are spending your most limited resource — time — to build awareness in people who have no immediate reason to call you.

A single well-placed article on a client's website once reached more than forty thousand readers in a month. One piece of content. Written once. Available to anyone who searched for it, at any hour, on any day.

That is the difference in scale. But scale alone is not the most important part.

\---

### High Intent vs. Broad Awareness

Not all digital channels work the same way, and understanding the difference matters for how you invest your time.

**Search engines — Google, AI assistants, Perplexity, Xiaohongshu — serve high-intent buyers.**

People do not wake up in the morning and casually search "how to divorce my husband." Nobody absent-mindedly wonders "what should I do if my company lorry crashes." These searches happen because something has already happened — a letter arrived, a relationship ended, an accident occurred, a job was suddenly gone.

Xiaohongshu deserves a specific mention here. Many people think of it as a lifestyle platform, but the behaviour on it is closer to a search engine — users open it with a specific topic in mind, type it in, and look for answers. Someone searching "employment rights Malaysia" or "how to find a good lawyer" on Xiaohongshu is not passively scrolling. They are looking. That intent is valuable, and it is an increasingly relevant channel for Malaysian professionals, particularly for reaching Chinese-speaking audiences.

The person behind any of these searches is in a moment of genuine need, actively looking for help right now. When your firm appears at the top of that result, you are not interrupting anyone. You are stepping forward at the exact moment they raised their hand.

**Social media — Instagram, Facebook — serves a different purpose.**

These platforms are where people go to be entertained and to follow topics they care about. The reader who sees your post explaining what "constructive dismissal" means is not necessarily going through a dismissal right now. But they are building familiarity with your name. When the situation arises — for them or someone they know — that familiarity pays off.

Search captures demand that already exists. Social media builds awareness that pays off later.

Both have a role in a complete digital strategy. But for a firm starting from zero, search is where you will see the fastest, most direct return. The person who finds you on Google has a problem today. That is where the cases come from.

\---

Your website works while you sleep. Your Google Business Profile answers questions at 3am when a panicked client is searching for help. Your content puts your name in front of someone in Kota Kinabalu who has never heard of you — at the exact moment they need you most.

This is what this book is about. Not visibility for its own sake. Visibility at the right moment, in front of the right person, when they are ready to call.

## Chapter 2: Authority vs Power

There is a concept from Alex Hormozi that I keep returning to when I talk to lawyers about content. He draws a distinction between two things most people treat as the same.

**Authority** is the right to be heard. A law degree gives you authority. Being called to the Bar gives you authority. A firm with 400 lawyers and four decades of history has authority. It is a credential — conferred by institutions, built over time, and largely static.

**Power** is different. Power is not given. It is earned through a specific kind of transaction.

Here is how Hormozi explains it. He watches Dr. Andrew Huberman — a Stanford neuroscientist and one of the most followed health communicators in the world. In one episode, Huberman mentions a technique for stopping hiccups. Specific. Actionable. Hormozi tries it. It works. From that moment, something shifts. *He told me to do something. I did it. It worked.* The next time Huberman makes a recommendation, Hormozi does not just passively listen — he is already primed to act. Trust has been proven, not assumed.

This is power. Not authority based on size or credentials, but influence earned through a simple transaction: *I gave you advice, you acted on it, it worked, and now you will listen to what I say next.*

\---

Now apply this to the legal market in Malaysia.

A client is negotiating a partnership agreement. They are not sure what to look for, what clauses are dangerous, where they are exposed. They search online and find a solo lawyer — one person, a modest practice — who has published a detailed, plain-language guide on exactly this: how to review a partnership agreement properly, what the red flags are, what to push back on.

They follow the advice. They go into the negotiation better prepared. They catch something they would have missed. It works.

On the other side of that search result is a firm with 400 lawyers, a prestigious address in the city centre, and decades of institutional history. Their website says they handle corporate work. There is no content. No guidance. Nothing that helps this client understand their situation before they spend money on a consultation.

Who does this client call?

The solo lawyer. Every time.

Not because the solo lawyer has more authority. The big firm has far more authority. But the solo lawyer has done something the big firm has not: they have already helped this client, before any money changed hands, before any phone call was made. They have demonstrated competence in a way the client could feel directly.

This is the transaction that builds power. And it is available to any lawyer who is willing to share their knowledge openly.

The same dynamic plays out in employment law. A worker receives what they believe is an unfair dismissal. They find an article from a solo practitioner walking through exactly what unfair dismissal means under Malaysian law, what the Industrial Relations process looks like, and what evidence to gather from day one. They follow the steps. When they eventually call a lawyer, they are not calling the biggest firm they can find — they are calling the one who already helped them.

**Authority says: I have the right to advise you.**

**Power says: I told you something. You did it. It worked. Now you will call me.**

The large firms have authority. A solo practitioner with a consistent content practice can build power that authority alone cannot buy. And in the search-driven world of 2026, power is what gets the phone to ring.

## Chapter 3: The Door Just Opened

For most of Malaysian legal history, this conversation would have been purely theoretical.

The Bar Council of Malaysia maintained strict rules about legal advertising. Soliciting business was prohibited. Websites had to be minimal. Any self-promotion that crossed certain lines was a disciplinary matter. In practice, this meant that Malaysian lawyers competed almost entirely on reputation and referral — a fundamentally word-of-mouth industry in an increasingly digital world.

This was not without its logic. There is a genuine concern in the legal profession about advertising that misleads clients, that commoditises something as serious as legal representation, or that puts pressure on lawyers to promise outcomes they cannot guarantee.

But there is a cost to invisibility that goes beyond the firms themselves — it falls on ordinary Malaysians.

I watch a lot of American content. And one thing strikes me consistently: ordinary people in the US know their rights. Not lawyers — regular people. They know the Fifth Amendment. They know what to say, and more importantly what *not* to say, when a police officer stops them. They know what an employer can and cannot legally do. They know when they are being pushed around and exactly how to push back. This knowledge is everywhere — on YouTube, in podcasts, in content that lawyers produce freely and openly because they are allowed to.

In Malaysia, decades of strict advertising rules did not just limit how lawyers could grow their practices. They kept legal knowledge locked inside the profession. The ordinary Malaysian who got a letter from their employer, or was pressured to sign something they did not understand, or was caught in a situation where someone with legal knowledge was taking advantage of them — they often had no accessible, clear, free resource to turn to. They did not know their rights because nobody was allowed to tell them.

That is the real cost of invisibility. Not just invisible firms. Invisible rights.

It is good and bad, of course. An informed public means more people who understand enough to question, to negotiate, to push back. Some lawyers will find that uncomfortable. But the reality is that a client who understands their situation is easier to serve, quicker to trust you, and far less likely to have unrealistic expectations about what you can achieve for them.

The Bar Council's decision to open up legal marketing is not just good for law firms. It is good for Malaysia.

That changed in 2026.

The Bar Council began loosening its advertising restrictions, opening the door to a more modern approach to legal marketing. The specifics matter less than the principle: for the first time in a generation, Malaysian lawyers can actively build a public presence, share their expertise, and be found by people who need them.

This is not a small thing. This is a once-in-a-generation opportunity.

And here is why the timing matters more than you might realise.

\---

**First-mover advantage is real, and it compounds.**

When a law firm builds a strong Google Business Profile, earns 50 reviews, and publishes consistent, helpful content — Google and AI systems learn to trust that firm. They start recommending it. The firm gets visibility. Visibility generates more clients. More clients generate more reviews. More reviews generate more visibility.

This is a flywheel. It is self-reinforcing. And right now, in 2026, most Malaysian law firms have not started it yet.

The competitor who starts today will be three years ahead of the one who starts in 2029. Not because they worked harder. Because they started earlier.

Digital presence compounds like interest. A firm that starts building in 2026 does not just have a slight advantage in 2030 — they have an enormous one, because every month of consistent presence adds to a foundation that took years to build.

Your competitors are still sitting on the sideline. Some of them are waiting to see if this marketing thing is "appropriate." Some of them are busy. Some of them do not know where to start.

A battery that sells for RM250 on Shopee — he sells for RM350. Higher price, and he still wins the customer. How?

The first phone that answers and quotes the fastest, wins. Every time. Shopee cannot compete with that, because Shopee requires the customer to have the luxury of time. Someone has just received a letter of demand. Someone's business partner has just announced they are leaving and taking clients. Someone has just been served with divorce papers. Someone's company lorry was involved in an accident this morning.

This makes intuitive sense. If you ask ChatGPT to recommend a restaurant, it is not going to pull information from the restaurant's own website — it is going to pull from reviews, food blogs, and platforms where real people have shared their real experiences. Law firms work the same way.

## Chapter 5: Google Is Your 24/7 Receptionist

Everything that follows in this book is actionable. Not theoretical, not aspirational — things you can do this week.

We are going to keep this focused. There are dozens of platforms where you could theoretically invest your time. We are going to talk about the two that matter most for Malaysian lawyers in 2026: **Google** and **AI search**. Master these two, and you will be ahead of 90% of your competition.

Let us start with Google. Specifically, Google Business Profile.

\---

**What is Google Business Profile?**

It is the information box that appears on the right-hand side (or at the top on mobile) when someone searches for your firm by name, or when someone searches for "lawyer near me" in your area. It shows your firm's name, address, phone number, hours, reviews, photos, and a brief description.

It is completely free. It is extremely powerful. And the vast majority of Malaysian law firms have either not claimed it, or claimed it and left it half-empty.

This is an enormous opportunity.

When someone searches "employment lawyer Petaling Jaya," Google does not just show website links — it shows a "local pack," a map with three firms highlighted. The firms in that local pack get the most clicks, the most calls, and the most cases. Getting into that local pack is one of the highest-leverage things a Malaysian law firm can do right now.

\---

**How to optimize your Google Business Profile:**

**Claim and verify it.** If you have not done this, go to business.google.com and claim your listing. Google will send a verification code to your firm's address. This takes a week or two, but it is the foundation of everything.

**Fill in every field.** Business name (exactly as your firm is registered). Address. Phone number. Website. Hours. Practice areas. Description. Do not leave anything blank. Each empty field is a missed signal.

**Write a compelling description.** Two to three sentences that explain who you serve, what you specialise in, and why clients choose you. Do not stuff it with keywords. Write it for a human being who is frightened and looking for help.

**Add photos.** A photo of your office exterior (so clients can find you). A photo of the interior. Professional photos of the solicitors. Do not use stock photography — real photos of real places build real trust.

**Add your practice areas.** Google allows you to categorise your services. Be specific. "Employment law," "family law," "corporate litigation" — these help Google understand what you do and match you to relevant searches.

\---

**Reviews: the single most important trust signal you have.**

If there is one thing you take from this chapter, let it be this: Google reviews are the closest thing to a superpower that a Malaysian law firm can have right now.

Here is why.

When Nurul searches for an employment lawyer and sees one firm with 47 reviews averaging 4.9 stars, and another firm with no reviews at all — she calls the first firm. Every time. The reviews are not just social proof for human readers. They are also one of the most powerful signals that Google and AI systems use to assess credibility.

Google reads your reviews. AI systems read your reviews. They look for patterns — for specific mentions of practice areas, for language like "professional," "knowledgeable," "resolved my case," "highly recommend." These phrases feed into how AI understands what your firm does and how well it does it.

**How to get reviews ethically:**

After a matter concludes well, send your client a simple message: *"Thank you for trusting us with your case. If you found our service helpful, we would be grateful if you could leave us a Google review — it helps other people in similar situations find us."* Include a direct link to your Google review page.

That is it. No incentives. No pressure. Just a polite ask at the right moment.

Most people who have had a good experience are happy to share it — they just do not think to do so without being asked. A system that sends this message after every successful matter, consistently, will build your review profile faster than you think.

\---

**Consistency is invisible but critical.**

Your firm's name, address, and phone number need to be exactly the same everywhere they appear online. Your website. Your Google Business Profile. Any directories you are listed in. The Malaysian Bar's online directory. LinkedIn. Everywhere.

If your firm is "Ng \& Associates" on your website but "Ng and Associates" in your Google listing and "Ng Associates" in a directory — AI systems see three different entities. This confusion weakens your digital credibility. Consistency tells AI: *this is one firm, they are established, they can be trusted.*

It sounds trivial. It is not. Fix it everywhere.

## Chapter 6: Getting AI to Recommend You

Google Business Profile handles your local search presence. But there is a second, rapidly growing channel that you cannot afford to ignore: AI search.

ChatGPT. Perplexity. Google's AI Overview. Microsoft Copilot. These tools are increasingly how people start their search for professional services. And they work differently from traditional search engines in one important way: they do not just show links. They make recommendations.

When someone asks ChatGPT *"who is a good family lawyer in Subang Jaya?"* — it answers. With names. With reasons. With a level of confidence that makes most people take the recommendation seriously.

The question is: whose name comes up?

\---

**How AI decides who to recommend.**

Chris Dreyer, founder of Rankings.io and author of the US legal marketing guide that inspired this book, has studied this question in depth. His team has observed thousands of AI-driven legal searches and reverse-engineered the signals that drive recommendations.

The finding is consistent: AI does not evaluate what you say about yourself. It evaluates what others say about you.

This is not an accident. AI systems are trained to behave like thoughtful human researchers. A thoughtful researcher does not just read a law firm's own website and take it at face value. They look for external validation — reviews, third-party mentions, directory listings, awards, case outcomes cited in public sources.

When the internet consistently describes a firm in specific, credible, detailed ways — from multiple independent sources — AI absorbs that as evidence of authority.

When a firm's only online presence is their own website saying "we are experienced and professional" — AI has almost nothing to work with.

The goal is to build what Dreyer calls an **entity** — a rich, consistent, multi-source digital identity that AI can understand, trust, and recommend.

\---

**The five things AI needs to recommend you:**

**1. A clear, structured website.**

Your website is the foundation. Each practice area needs its own page — not a paragraph under "services," but a dedicated page with a clear title, a description of what you do, who you help, and how the process works.

When someone types "employment lawyer Malaysia" into an AI system, it is looking for pages that clearly say: *this firm handles employment law, this is what that means, this is how it works, this is who handles it.* Give AI the structure it needs to understand you.

**2. Content that answers real questions.**

The single most effective thing you can do for both Google and AI is to write articles that answer the questions your clients actually ask.

*"What are my rights if my employer makes me redundant?"
"How long does a divorce take in Malaysia?"
"Can my landlord keep my deposit?"*

These are questions that real Malaysians type into Google and ask AI every day. A firm that has clear, well-written articles answering these questions is being *found* at the exact moment of maximum intent — when someone has a problem and is actively looking for help.

You do not need to publish daily. Four articles per month, each answering one genuine question clearly and thoroughly, will compound over time into a significant content library.

And to return to the event planner story: do not be afraid that explaining these things will make people handle their own cases. It will not. It will make them trust you enough to call.

**3. Consistent information everywhere.**

As discussed in the previous chapter: your name, address, and phone number must be identical everywhere online. Your website. Google Business Profile. The Malaysian Bar's online directory. LinkedIn. Any other directory or platform where you appear.

Dreyer's research is unequivocal on this: consistency strengthens your entity. Inconsistency makes AI hesitate, and when AI hesitates, it recommends someone else.

**4. Reviews with substance.**

A review that says "Great lawyer, very helpful" is better than nothing. A review that says *"Jacob helped me navigate a wrongful dismissal case. He explained every step clearly, was responsive to all my questions, and we won the case at the industrial court. I highly recommend him to anyone facing an employment issue"* is worth ten of the former.

Specific, detailed reviews that mention practice areas, outcomes, and your personal qualities give AI genuine material to work with. Encourage your clients to be specific when they review you.

**5. Third-party mentions and citations.**

AI trusts information that comes from outside your firm more than information that comes from inside it. This means getting your firm mentioned in news articles, industry roundups, professional directories, or any credible third-party platform.

For Malaysian lawyers, the most accessible starting point is the Malaysian Bar's online directory — ensure your profile is complete and accurate. From there, look for any legitimate legal directory that lists Malaysian solicitors, and ensure your presence is complete on each.

Over time, if you publish good content, local media and legal publications may cite you. Each citation is another signal to AI that you are a credible authority.

\---

**A note on patience.**

Building AI visibility is not a campaign with a clear end date. It is more like tending a garden. The signals you build today — the reviews, the content, the consistent citations — accumulate over months and years into something that becomes very difficult for competitors to replicate quickly.

Dreyer's team documented a case where a firm added structured case results pages to their website. Within a week, AI systems began citing those specific pages in their responses to client queries. The speed of change can surprise you.

But consistency is the non-negotiable. Firms that build steadily, month after month, are the ones that end up being recommended first. Not because they did anything miraculous — but because they showed up, repeatedly, over time.

### Building Your Website: The Tools That Changed Everything

Until recently, building a professional website required either significant technical knowledge or a significant budget for a web developer. A good developer might charge RM5,000 to RM15,000 for a professional law firm website. A really good one, with high-quality design, might charge RM20,000 or more.

## Chapter 8: Don't Build on Broken Ground

I need to address something directly before we go further.

For the last decade, the default answer to "how do I build a website?" has been WordPress. It is the most widely used content management system in the world. Millions of websites run on it. There are thousands of themes and plugins. Tutorials everywhere. Developers who know it inside out.

I am not going to tell you WordPress cannot do the job. It can. A properly built, well-maintained WordPress site can be a perfectly functional professional platform.

But I am going to tell you something that I have seen play out in practice, over and over again, with clients who came to me after spending months firefighting problems that should never have existed in the first place.

**WordPress is one of the most-hacked platforms on the internet.**

\---

### The Real Cost of a Hacked Website

The nature of hacking has changed. It is no longer primarily about defacing websites or stealing data — though that still happens. The most common attack vector against small business WordPress sites in 2026 is **crypto mining injection**.

Here is what that means in practice.

Someone exploits a vulnerability in your WordPress installation — perhaps an outdated plugin, a weak password, a theme with a known security flaw. They inject malicious code into your website. This code uses your server's computing power to mine cryptocurrency for someone else. Your server is now doing work for a stranger, completely without your knowledge.

The symptoms? Your website becomes slow. Sometimes extremely slow. Pages time out. The server crashes under load. Your hosting provider may suspend your account. Images stop loading. The site goes down entirely.

And you — a lawyer who should be spending your time on client work — are now spending hours, days, or even weeks trying to figure out what happened. Or paying someone else to figure it out. Or both.

I have seen this scenario more times than I can count. Lawyers, professionals, small business owners who came to me not for digital strategy but for emergency recovery. *"My website is broken. Images are crashing. The server keeps going down. I don't know what's happening."*

When I look at the site: WordPress. Old plugins. Outdated theme. Injected code running in the background, silently consuming their server resources while their clients try to load the homepage and get a timeout error instead.

\---

### The Maintenance Burden Nobody Talks About

Even without a hack, WordPress requires ongoing maintenance. Regular updates to the core software. Plugin compatibility checks every time something updates. Backup management. Security scanning. Theme updates that sometimes break your layout entirely and require a developer to fix.

For a developer who does this professionally, it is manageable. For a busy lawyer who just wants their website to work — it is a constant, low-grade source of anxiety and expense.

The modern AI-powered tools I described in the previous chapter — Lovable, Bolt.new, Figma Make — do not have this problem. They generate static sites or modern hosted applications. There are no plugins to update. No database to secure. No injection vulnerabilities from outdated themes. The attack surface is dramatically smaller.

Your time is worth too much to spend it solving WordPress problems. Build on a foundation that does not crack.

\---

### If You Are Already on WordPress

If your firm already has a WordPress site that is actively maintained by a competent developer, updated regularly, and backed up properly — this chapter is not a reason to panic. A well-maintained WordPress site is fine.

But if your WordPress site was set up two years ago by someone who has not touched it since, or if you are the one maintaining it without a clear security and backup strategy — I would encourage you to consider rebuilding on more modern infrastructure. ## Chapter 9: Your Domain, Your Digital Address

Before your website can exist on the internet, you need a domain name. This is the address people type to find you — *yourfirm.com*, or *yourfirm.my*, or *yourfirm.com.my*.

\---

### Choosing Your Domain Extension

The extension — the part after the dot — matters more for perception than for technical function. Here is a simple guide:

**For international-facing firms and general use: .com**

A .com is universally recognised, easy to remember, and carries no geographic implication. If you want a domain that works for any audience anywhere, .com is the default choice. It is the most trusted extension globally.

**For Malaysian-specific presence: .my**

Here is something that surprises many people: a .my domain — the Malaysian country code — is extremely affordable. On Namecheap, you can register a .my domain for approximately USD 2 per year. Two dollars. For a professional domain that signals Malaysian presence, that is extraordinary value.

There is sometimes a perception that a .my domain is somehow "less professional" than a .com. This is simply not true. For a Malaysian law firm serving Malaysian clients, a .my domain is entirely appropriate and costs almost nothing to register.

**For .com.my: only if you need it**

The .com.my extension explicitly marks the site as a Malaysian commercial entity and comes with specific eligibility requirements. It is handled through local registrars and is slightly more involved to register.

My honest view: you do not need to spend extra money on .com.my unless you have a specific reason to. A .my or .com domain works perfectly well, costs less, and in many cases registers more easily.

## Chapter 11: Writing Blogs That Actually Bring Cases

Your website is live. Your Google Business Profile is claimed. Your reviews are beginning to accumulate.

Now comes the part that separates the firms that stay visible from the firms that fade back into the background.

Content.

Specifically: blog articles that are not generic, not boring, and not written for no one in particular. Articles that are written with a strategic understanding of exactly who you are trying to reach — and exactly what they are thinking when they turn to Google or an AI for help.

\---

### The CEP Framework — What I Learned from a PhD in Marketing

I want to share a concept that changed how I think about content strategy. I picked it up from a conversation with someone who holds a doctorate in marketing — someone who has spent their career studying how consumers connect with brands and make purchase decisions.

The concept is called **Category Entry Points**, or CEPs.

A Category Entry Point is a specific situation, moment, or context that triggers someone to seek help in a particular category. It is the answer to the question: *"What is the person thinking, doing, or experiencing at the exact moment they decide they need a lawyer?"*

When you understand your CEPs, you stop writing generic content ("what is personal injury law?") and start writing content that meets people where they actually are ("what should I do immediately after a lorry hit my car on the highway?").

The difference between those two types of content is the difference between a library and a lifeline.

\---

### How to Find Your CEPs Using AI

Here is a practical exercise. Open Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini — whichever you are most comfortable with — and type this prompt:

*"Come up with 20 Category Entry Points for a personal injury lawyer's marketing. These should be the specific situations, moments, and search behaviours that indicate someone needs a personal injury lawyer right now."*

The AI will generate a long list. Some of them will be obvious. Some will surprise you. Some will open strategic conversations you have never had before.

But here is what I want you to do with that list: **choose**.

This is the mistake I see most often when I work with lawyers on their content strategy. They look at a list of 20 potential directions and say *"I want to reach everyone."* And so they write content that reaches no one in particular.

Every firm has limited time and limited energy for content creation. The firms that win are not the ones that cover the most ground. They are the ones that dig deepest in the ground that matters most to them.

\---

### The Quality of the Lead Depends on the Entry Point You Target

Let me illustrate this with something concrete from personal injury law, because it demonstrates the strategic dimension of CEP selection better than almost anything else I could show you.

If you are a personal injury lawyer and you write content targeting **motorcycle accidents** — one of the most common road accident categories in Malaysia — you will generate leads. Consistent ones. Malaysian roads have a significant number of motorcycle accidents. The audience is there.

But the nature of those cases, and the likely value of that work, will sit in a certain range.

Now consider a different CEP: **lorry accidents on the highway**.

A commercial lorry is not just a vehicle. It is an asset. The lorry itself might be worth hundreds of thousands of ringgit. The goods it transports could be worth more. The company operating the lorry has commercial insurance. The insurance company has resources, and they have lawyers.

When someone is injured in a lorry accident and they are looking at a potential claim against a logistics company and its insurer — that is a fundamentally different category of case. The potential damages are different. The complexity is different. The likely settlement value is different. And the client who comes to you through that entry point is not shopping for the cheapest lawyer they can find — they are looking for someone capable enough to fight.

**Both entry points are valid.** I want to be clear about that.

Some firms thrive on high volume, consistent, lower-complexity personal injury work. They have systems for it. They are good at it. The economics work well for them, and they serve a real and important market. There is absolutely nothing wrong with targeting motorcycle accident content if that is the practice profile you want.

But if you are a firm that wants fewer, higher-value cases — the client who is looking at a potentially significant payout and needs a firm they can trust to handle it properly — then the entry point you target in your content determines the client who finds you.

Choose deliberately. Not out of snobbery about case types, but out of clarity about what kind of firm you want to be.

\---

### CEP Examples by Practice Area

Here are some starting points across common practice areas to spark your thinking.

**Employment Law**

* Received a show-cause letter unexpectedly and don't know how to respond
* Made redundant during a company restructuring
* Employer withholding final salary or EPF contributions after resignation
* Asked to resign "voluntarily" under pressure — is this constructive dismissal?
* Contract worker whose contract was suddenly terminated before the agreed end date

**Family Law**

* Spouse has moved money out of joint accounts before filing for divorce
* Child custody dispute following a bitter separation
* Foreign national married to a Malaysian — which court has jurisdiction?
* Non-Muslim couple navigating property division after a long separation

**Property and Construction**

* Developer delivered property years past the promised handover date
* Renovation contractor abandoned the project halfway through
* Landlord refusing to return deposit without a legitimate reason
* Neighbour's illegal renovation causing structural damage to your unit

**Personal Injury**

* Lorry accident on the highway — assessing liability and insurance claims
* Industrial accident at the workplace — was the employer negligent?
* Medical procedure that resulted in a significantly worse outcome than expected
* Slip and fall at a commercial premises — what is the owner's liability?

Notice something about this list. These are not "what is employment law" articles. These are articles written for a specific person in a specific moment. That specificity is what makes them findable — and what makes them convert.

\---

### How to Write the Article Using AI

Once you have identified a CEP, open Claude or Gemini and use a prompt like this:

*"I am a Malaysian employment lawyer based in Petaling Jaya. Write a blog article for someone who has just received a show-cause letter from their employer and doesn't know what to do. The article should explain what a show-cause letter is, what rights the employee has, what the deadlines are, and what mistakes people commonly make when responding. Write it in plain English that a non-lawyer can understand, with warmth and clarity. Around 700 to 900 words."*

The AI will draft the article. Read it carefully — you know the law, the AI does not always get it right. Correct any inaccuracies. Add your voice. Add a real observation from your practice (anonymised). And publish it.

That is your content process. One article per week, or even one per fortnight, each targeting a specific CEP. Over six months, you will have a library of content that covers the situations your clients are actually in, that appears in search results and AI responses, and that builds your authority with every person who reads it.

\---

### Which AI Should You Use for Writing?

Both **Claude** (claude.ai) and **Gemini** (gemini.google.com) are excellent for this kind of content work.

Claude tends to produce writing that is more natural and nuanced — it understands tone well and produces content that reads like it was written by a thoughtful human rather than a machine. For law firm blog articles, where warmth and clarity matter as much as accuracy, Claude is my preference.

Gemini integrates well with Google's ecosystem — if you are already working in Google Docs or Workspace, the integration is seamless. And if you are a Gemini Pro subscriber, the full capability is already included in what you are paying for.

My recommendation: try both on a few articles and use whichever produces output you feel most comfortable editing into your own voice. The tool matters less than the habit. Write consistently, target your CEPs deliberately, and the results will follow.

## Chapter 12: Google Search Console — Tell Google You Exist

There is a step that most small business owners and professionals skip entirely when they launch a new website. They build the site, they publish it, they share the link with a few people — and then they wait for Google to notice them.

Google will eventually find your website on its own. Crawlers scan the internet constantly. But "eventually" can mean weeks or months. And in a market where first-mover advantage is everything, weeks and months of invisibility are weeks and months of missed opportunities.

There is a better way: **tell Google your website exists, directly**.

\---

### What Is Google Search Console?

Google Search Console is a free tool provided by Google that lets you manage your website's presence in Google Search. It is entirely separate from Google Business Profile, which handles your local "map pack" presence. Search Console handles your organic search presence — what appears when someone searches without a location qualifier.

When you submit your website to Google Search Console, you are essentially introducing yourself. You are saying: *here is my website, here is how it is structured, here are the pages you should know about.* Google's crawlers then visit your site with priority, understand it more quickly, and begin surfacing it in relevant search results faster.

\---

### How to Submit Your Website — Step by Step

The process takes about 15 minutes and requires no technical knowledge:

1. Go to **search.google.com/search-console** and sign in with your Google account.
2. Click "Add Property" and enter your website's full URL (e.g., https://yourfirm.my).
3. Google will ask you to verify that you own the website. The simplest method is to download a small HTML file and upload it to your site — Vercel and Netlify both make this straightforward. Alternatively, your domain registrar can help you add a DNS verification record.
4. Once verified, go to "Sitemaps" in the left menu and submit your sitemap URL. Most modern website builders — including sites built with Figma Make and published on Vercel — generate a sitemap automatically at **yourfirm.my/sitemap.xml**.
5. Use the "URL Inspection" tool to submit your homepage and each of your main service pages individually for crawling.

That is it. Google will now crawl your site with priority and begin indexing your pages.

\---

### What Search Console Tells You

Beyond the initial submission, Google Search Console becomes one of your most valuable ongoing tools. It shows you:

* **Which searches your site is appearing in** — the actual queries people are typing that cause your site to appear, even when they do not click through.
* **How many clicks you are receiving** from organic search, and which pages are performing best.
* **Which pages Google has indexed** — and more importantly, which pages it has not, so you can investigate any technical issues.
* **Errors and warnings** — if Google encounters problems crawling your site, it reports them here so you can fix them.

For a law firm just beginning to build its digital presence, Search Console provides a window into something that was previously invisible: what people are actually searching for when they find you. This data is gold for refining your content strategy and understanding which CEPs are driving real traffic.

\---

### The Difference Between Existing and Being Found

Publishing your website does not mean Google knows it exists. Having a Google Business Profile does not automatically connect Google to your website's deeper content. These are separate systems, and you need to feed each of them the right signals.

Think of it this way. You have opened a new office. You have put up a sign outside. But if you have not listed the address anywhere, told anyone you have opened, or registered with any directory — how does anyone know to come?

Google Search Console is the process of registering your existence with the most important directory in the world. It takes 15 minutes. It is free. And it turns your website from a file that exists somewhere on a server into a document that Google actively knows about, understands, and can serve to people who need what you offer.

Do it the day you launch. Do not wait.
